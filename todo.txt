5.8) Storage capacity estimation for LibreMesh deployment (planning task)
     Time estimate: ~1 hour (calculation and documentation)
     - Purpose: determine realistic capacity, node architecture, and bandwidth considerations
       for a bootstrapped LibreMesh deployment with limited budget and single-location constraint.
     
     DEPLOYMENT CONTEXT:
     - Budget constraint: minimize upfront costs, reuse hardware if project fails
     - Location: single site initially (rented rack space or home)
     - Connection: symmetric 50 Mbit/s (6.25 MB/s) = ~540 GB/day theoretical
     - Hardware: Raspberry Pi 5 with SSD HAT + USB3 HDD storage
     
     BANDWIDTH ANALYSIS (50 Mbit/s symmetric):
     - Raw throughput: 6.25 MB/s = 22.5 GB/hour = 540 GB/day
     - With Reed-Solomon overhead (k=6, n=10 = 1.67x): 
         - Ingress capacity: ~320 GB/day usable data (needs 540 GB transferred)
         - Egress capacity: ~320 GB/day served (needs 540 GB transferred)
     - Monthly bandwidth: ~9.6 TB ingress + 9.6 TB egress = 19.2 TB total
     - Bottleneck: bandwidth is the limiting factor, not storage
     - Reality check: 50% utilization = ~160 GB/day = 4.8 TB/month usable
     
     STORJ COMPARISON:
     - Storj total: ~4.5 exabytes across ~12,000 nodes (~375 TB average/node)
     - Storj home nodes: typically 1-8 TB with varied bandwidth
     - Storj pricing: ~$4/TB/month storage, $7/TB egress
     - LibreMesh positioning: much smaller scale, bootstrap/proof-of-concept
     
     OTHER DISTRIBUTED STORAGE COMPARISONS:
     - Sia: ~2-3 PB total, $1-2/TB/month, similar home node model
     - Filecoin: enterprise/datacenter focus, higher costs
     - Tardigrade (Storj): 150+ nodes minimum for production
     - Reality: bootstrapping with <10 nodes is experimental, not production-ready
     
     ARCHITECTURE: ONE BIG vs MULTIPLE SMALL NODES
     - One big node (8-12 TB):
         + Simpler management, single purchase
         + Lower power consumption (one Pi vs multiple)
         - Single point of failure (entire location down = total loss)
         - Cannot take advantage of Reed-Solomon redundancy within location
         - All eggs in one basket if project fails or drive fails
     
     - Multiple small nodes (2-4 TB each, 3-4 nodes):
         + Can lose 1-2 nodes and still serve data (if k=6, n=10, need 6 of 10)
         + Easier to start small (2TB) and add capacity incrementally
         + If project fails, smaller drives easier to repurpose for personal use
         + Better simulates multi-node deployment for testing
         + Can spread across racks/shelves for minor fault isolation
         - More power consumption (3-4 Pis vs 1)
         - More management overhead (4 configs, 4 OS updates, etc.)
     
     RECOMMENDATION: Start with 3 small satellites (2-4 TB each)
     
     REALISTIC STARTING CAPACITY (bandwidth-constrained):
     - Bandwidth limit: 50 Mbit/s = ~4.8 TB/month usable @ 50% utilization
     - Storage should match ~3-6 months of bandwidth capacity
     - Recommended: 10-20 TB raw storage total
     - Configuration:
         - Origin: 256GB SSD (boot/OS) + 500GB SSD (repair temp) = no fragment storage
         - 3 satellites: 128GB SSD (boot/OS) + 4TB HDD each = 12TB raw
         - With RS overhead (1.67x): 12TB raw = ~7.2 TB usable
         - Result: ~7TB usable storage, limited by bandwidth not capacity
     
     STORAGE CALCULATIONS:
     - Minimum bootstrap (10TB raw across 3 satellites):
         - Reed-Solomon: k=6, n=10 → need at least 6 satellites for single failure tolerance
         - With only 3 satellites: can only do k=2, n=3 (lose 1 satellite = still works)
         - 10TB raw ÷ 1.5x (k=2,n=3) = ~6.7TB usable
         - Per-satellite: 3.3TB average (use 4TB drives with headroom)
     
     - Medium deployment (30TB raw across 6 satellites):
         - Reed-Solomon: k=4, n=6 → lose 2 satellites, still serve data
         - 30TB raw ÷ 1.5x = 20TB usable
         - Per-satellite: 5TB average (use 6TB drives)
         - Origin repair temp: 1TB SSD (20% of raw for concurrent repairs)
     
     - Full deployment (60TB raw across 10 satellites):
         - Reed-Solomon: k=6, n=10 → lose 4 satellites, still serve data
         - 60TB raw ÷ 1.67x = ~36TB usable
         - Per-satellite: 6TB average (use 8TB drives with headroom)
         - Origin repair temp: 2TB SSD
     
     COST ESTIMATION (bootstrap 3-satellite setup):
     - 3× Raspberry Pi 5 (4GB): ~$180 (3 × $60)
     - 3× Official Pi 5 power supply: ~$75 (3 × $25)
     - 3× Pi 5 M.2 HAT+: ~$36 (3 × $12)
     - 3× 128GB M.2 NVMe SSD: ~$60 (3 × $20)
     - 3× 4TB USB3 HDD: ~$270 (3 × $90)
     - 3× USB3 HDD enclosure: ~$60 (3 × $20)
     - 1× Origin Pi 5 (8GB): ~$80
     - 1× Origin 256GB SSD: ~$30
     - 1× Origin 500GB temp SSD: ~$50
     - Network gear (switch, cables): ~$50
     - Total: ~$891 for 3 satellites + origin (12TB raw = ~8TB usable)
     
     ONGOING COSTS:

13) Placement & Scheduling (geo-awareness, diversity, score-driven) — fairly complex, placement policy
   - Purpose: decide which storagenodes should hold which fragments based on score, health, geography, and diversity.
   - Inputs: storagenode composite score (task 10), fill %, health (SMART), latency, and geo/affinity tags (rack/region/zone/operator).
   - Diversity: spread fragments across distinct fault domains (rack/region/operator) to avoid correlated loss; enforce minimum zone/region count when choosing n.
   - Geo-awareness: prefer lower-latency/closer storagenodes when diversity constraints are satisfied.
   - Even-fill: bias toward least-full healthy storagenodes while honoring diversity and scores.
   - Score-driven: prefer high-score storagenodes; exclude below-threshold scores (task 10 rules).
   - Repair/rebalance: when diversity or fill skews, enqueue rebalance/repair jobs to restore targets.
   - Zones (default 12): NA-East, NA-West, South America, Europe-West, Europe-East, Africa, Middle East, South Asia, East Asia-North, East Asia-South, Oceania, Polar/Remote. Start with all 12; merge down to 4 if the mesh is very small.
   - Per-object policy: place fragments across at least 3 distinct zones (or min(3, available_zones)) and across distinct operators/racks when possible; avoid more than 50% of copies in a single zone.
   - Resource balancing (from notes): 
       - Even-fill: prefer least-full healthy storagenodes to keep fill percentage roughly uniform; keep per-node headroom for repairs/rebalance.
       - Per-feeder adaptive throttling: allow all feeders; enforce a rate budget with decay; sustained continuous streams get throttled to a crawl (no hard rejects).
       - Soft caps with decay/reset: bytes/day per feeder; after the cap, progressively slow; window reset restores normal speed.
       - Optional vetted-key fast lane: manually issued keys for known feeders bypass heavy throttling but still respect global backpressure.
       - Global backpressure: when free capacity is low or storagenodes near cap, slow everyone; cap concurrent GET/PUT per storagenode to protect egress.
   - Acceptance: fragments are placed with diversity and score preference; placement rebalances when skewed; throttling prevents abuse.
